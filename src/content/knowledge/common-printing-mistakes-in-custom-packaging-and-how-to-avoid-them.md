From color inconsistencies to layout errors, small mistakes can lead to costly reprints, delays, and disputes.

## 1. Assuming Screen Colors Will Match Printed Colors

One of the most common misunderstandings is expecting printed colors to look exactly the same as they do on a computer screen.

**Why this happens:**
- Screens display colors using RGB
- Printing uses CMYK inks
- Different printing machines and materials affect color appearance

**How to avoid it:**
- Always design in CMYK color mode
- Request a printed color proof when color accuracy is critical
- Accept a reasonable tolerance range for mass production

Understanding color limitations early can prevent unnecessary disputes later.

---

## 2. Ignoring Material Impact on Printing Results

The same artwork can look very different when printed on different materials.

Common factors include:
- Glossy vs matte film
- Paperboard vs laminated surfaces
- Presence of aluminum foil layers

**How to avoid it:**
- Confirm final material structure before artwork approval
- Ask for sample photos or physical samples printed on similar materials
- Avoid last-minute material changes after design approval

Material selection should always be finalized **before** color confirmation.

---

## 3. Placing Critical Text Too Close to Cutting Lines

Important information such as batch numbers, nutrition facts, or warnings is sometimes placed too close to trim or fold lines.

**Why this is risky:**
- Minor cutting deviations are normal in mass production
- Folding and gluing can slightly shift positions

**How to avoid it:**
- Follow dieline safety margins strictly
- Keep critical text away from edges and folds
- Review dielines carefully before approving artwork

This is especially important for regulated products.

---

## 4. Overlooking Small Text Readability

Designs often look clean on screen, but small text may become difficult to read after printing.

Common issues include:
- Thin fonts
- Light colors on dark backgrounds
- Small font sizes on textured materials

**How to avoid it:**
- Avoid extremely thin fonts for mandatory information
- Increase font size slightly for regulatory text
- Print test samples when readability is critical

Readability should never be sacrificed for visual style.

---

## 5. Forgetting Consistency Across Variants

For product lines with multiple flavors or variants, inconsistencies can easily occur.

Typical mistakes:
- Incorrect batch numbers
- Mismatched flavor names
- Different color tones across variants

**How to avoid it:**
- Create a clear master artwork reference
- Double-check variable elements separately
- Use a structured approval process for each SKU

Small inconsistencies can cause big issues in retail environments.

---

## 6. Rushing Artwork Approval to Meet Deadlines

Tight timelines often lead to rushed approvals, increasing the risk of errors.

**How to avoid it:**
- Allocate sufficient time for artwork review
- Involve both design and production teams
- Treat artwork approval as a final checkpoint, not a formality

Fixing issues before printing is always cheaper than correcting them afterward.

---

## Final Thoughts

Most printing mistakes are not caused by lack of effort, but by lack of **production awareness** during the design stage.

Working with an experienced packaging manufacturer who understands materials, printing processes, and export requirements can significantly reduce risks and ensure smoother production.
